# The Limits of Academia: On "Describing" a Complex World
**Date:** July 2, 2026

### Is "Scientific Fact" the Same as Reality?
As social science researchers, we often operate on the assumption that "society" is a solid, undeniable entity. We deploy various methodologies to uncover cause-and-effect relationships within it. However, a "scientific fact" does not always perfectly reflect the messy reality we actually live in.

After all, what exactly is "society"? The way we perceive it varies wildly from person to person. Yet, within the strict frameworks of science, these differences—this multiplicity—are often treated as irrational noise, cleanly stripped away by our research methods. Scholars like John Law and Annemarie Mol have long critiqued this exact approach to academia.

### The Dilemma of Seeking "Universal Truths"
Academic papers constantly demand that we find "universal facts" that apply to everyone. Anything lacking clear empirical data is quickly dismissed as "just a personal opinion" and denied the label of "science." This happens even though it's virtually impossible to make sweeping generalizations about what "society" truly is.

But what about the reality we experience every day? Our world is far more irrational, overflowing with things that completely defy logical explanation.

### Describing, Not Explaining

That is why I believe we need to step back from forcing simple "explanations" onto things, and instead focus on "describing" complex phenomena exactly as they are—in all their complexity.

Most importantly, when we encounter something that simply cannot be captured in words or data, we must stop forcing it into a neat little box and pretending, "This is a fact." We need the courage to honestly admit, "I cannot describe this."

Acknowledging our own limitations and continuing to face the unknown with sincerity—I believe this is the absolute core of honesty required of any researcher confronting our complex reality.
